A Swiss sole proprietorship (Einzelfirma) requires no minimum capital, no notary, and no Commercial Register entry for businesses below CHF 100,000 in annual turnover. It is the fastest and cheapest way to start a business in Switzerland. The trade-off is unlimited personal liability and a personal income tax rate that makes the GmbH more efficient once profit exceeds CHF 100,000–150,000/year. This guide covers registration, costs, tax obligations, and when to make the switch.

---

## What Is a Swiss Sole Proprietorship?

The sole proprietorship (Einzelfirma) is the simplest legal form under Swiss commercial law. It is not a separate legal entity — it is the same person as the owner, operating a business. This means:

- **No minimum capital required.** You can start with zero investment beyond the tools of your trade.
- **No notary required.** Formation requires no notarial deed and no formal articles of association.
- **No Commercial Register entry required** below CHF 100,000 annual turnover (though voluntary registration is possible).
- **Unlimited personal liability.** Every debt and obligation of the business is a personal debt and obligation of the owner.

The legal basis is the <a href="https://www.fedlex.admin.ch/eli/cc/27/317_321_377/en" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Swiss Code of Obligations (OR/CO)</a>, Article 945 and following, which governs the commercial register obligations of individual persons.

A sole proprietorship is the right structure for freelancers, consultants, craftspeople, and small-scale service providers who are starting out and whose annual revenue is likely to remain below CHF 100,000–150,000.

## Registration Requirements

**Below CHF 100,000 annual turnover:**

Registration with the cantonal Commercial Register is voluntary. The business can operate legally without any registration. If the business has a distinctive name (e.g. "Graphic Design Studio by Anna Keller"), the name must include the owner's surname and cannot mislead about the nature of the business.

Even below the threshold, registration is sometimes worthwhile:
- Improves credibility with clients and banks
- Enables the business to invoice under a registered name
- Makes it easier to open a business bank account

**Above CHF 100,000 annual turnover:**

Registration with the Commercial Register is mandatory under Article 957 CO. The registration must include the owner's surname, and the business name chosen must include that surname (e.g. "Müller Consulting" is valid; "Alpine Consulting" without a surname is not).

**EU/EFTA nationals** may register a sole proprietorship on the same basis as Swiss nationals under the Free Movement of Persons Agreement.

**Non-EU/EEA nationals** must have the right to work in Switzerland as self-employed (a B permit with self-employed status, or a C permit) before operating a sole proprietorship.

## How to Register an Einzelfirma

If turnover is below CHF 100,000 and voluntary registration is not sought, there is nothing to file. Simply begin operating and comply with applicable AHV and VAT obligations.

For mandatory registration (turnover exceeds CHF 100,000) or voluntary registration:

### Step 1: Choose a business name

The name must contain the owner's surname. Additional descriptive elements are permitted (e.g. "Keller Engineering Services"). Check <a href="https://www.zefix.ch" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">ZEFIX</a> to confirm the name is not already registered by another party.

### Step 2: Prepare the registration form

The cantonal Commercial Register provides a standard registration form. Required information:
- Owner's full name and date of birth
- Swiss address (place of domicile)
- Business name
- Registered office of the business
- Description of business purpose
- Bank account details (for future AHV collection)

### Step 3: Submit to the cantonal Commercial Register

Submit the completed form to the Commercial Register of the canton where the business is based. Most cantons now accept online or postal submission.

### Step 4: Receive registration confirmation and UID

The Commercial Register issues a UID (Unternehmens-Identifikationsnummer) — the Swiss business identification number. The business is then listed on ZEFIX and in the Official Gazette of Commerce (SHAB).

**Registration fee:** CHF 120 to CHF 180 depending on the canton.

**Processing time:** 1 to 2 weeks for most cantons.

## Costs of Setting Up

| Item | Cost |
|---|---|
| Commercial Register entry (mandatory above CHF 100,000) | CHF 120–180 |
| Business bank account setup | CHF 0–100 |
| AHV registration (mandatory for all self-employed) | Free (contributions payable on income) |
| VAT registration (mandatory above CHF 100,000 turnover) | Free to register |
| Accounting software | CHF 200–600/year |

The sole proprietorship has the lowest setup cost of any Swiss business structure. There is no share capital, no notary, and minimal administrative cost at formation.

Ongoing costs are driven primarily by accounting and tax filing. A sole proprietor below CHF 500,000 turnover can use simplified bookkeeping (income and expenditure account plus asset register), which reduces accounting costs significantly compared to a GmbH.

## Tax and Social Security Obligations

### Income tax

The sole proprietor's business profit is taxable as personal income in Switzerland. There is no separate corporate tax. The income is combined with any other personal income (salary, dividends, rental income) and taxed at the cantonal and federal progressive rate.

**Illustrative effective income tax rates on CHF 150,000 net income (2026 estimates):**

| Canton | Estimated effective income tax rate |
|---|---|
| Zug (city of Zug) | ~22% |
| Lucerne | ~26% |
| Bern | ~31% |
| Zurich (city) | ~27% |

At higher income levels (CHF 200,000+), the marginal rate approaches or exceeds 40% in high-tax cantons. This is the key driver for converting to a GmbH.

### AHV/IV/EO contributions

Every self-employed person must pay AHV/IV/EO (old-age insurance, disability insurance, earnings replacement) contributions on net business income. The contribution rate for self-employed persons is approximately 10% on net income.

Registration with the cantonal AHV compensation fund (Ausgleichskasse) is mandatory. Contributions are assessed annually based on the previous year's income and paid in quarterly instalments.

### VAT

A sole proprietor whose worldwide taxable turnover exceeds CHF 100,000 must register for Swiss VAT (MWST). The standard VAT rate is 8.1%. For more detail, see our [Swiss VAT registration guide](/tax-accounting/swiss-vat-registration/).

## Unlimited Liability: The Main Risk

The most significant disadvantage of the sole proprietorship is unlimited personal liability. Unlike a GmbH or AG, where the company's liability is limited to its assets, the sole proprietor is personally responsible for every obligation of the business.

This means:
- A business debt can be enforced against the owner's personal bank accounts, real estate, vehicles, and other personal assets
- A business lawsuit is a personal lawsuit
- Business insolvency typically means personal insolvency

For service businesses with minimal capital assets and clients who pay on time, unlimited liability is manageable. For businesses that:
- Hold significant inventory or equipment
- Enter into large contracts with financial penalties
- Work in sectors with significant professional liability exposure
- Employ staff (employment law liabilities)

... the GmbH's liability shield becomes materially valuable.

## When to Convert from Einzelfirma to GmbH

The decision to convert from an Einzelfirma to a GmbH depends on four factors:

**1. Tax efficiency.** The combined corporate tax and dividend tax burden of a GmbH typically becomes lower than the combined income tax and AHV contributions of a sole proprietor at CHF 100,000–150,000 in annual net profit. The exact break-even point depends on the canton, the owner's family situation, and dividend policy. A cantonal tax calculation is essential before deciding.

**2. Liability protection.** If the business involves significant contracts, employees, or professional liability exposure, the GmbH's limited liability becomes increasingly important.

**3. Business credibility.** For certain clients and sectors — financial services, regulated activities, large corporate counterparties — operating through a GmbH rather than a sole proprietorship may be expected or required.

**4. Future plans.** If external investors, venture capital, or future sale of the business is planned, the GmbH (or AG) is the right structure from the start. Converting later involves notarial costs and additional formation complexity.

**Conversion process:**

An existing sole proprietorship can be converted to a GmbH by contributing the business as a contribution in kind (Sacheinlage) to a newly formed GmbH. This requires a notarial deed, a creditor notification period, and Commercial Register filings. Costs: CHF 3,000–6,000 in professional fees plus the CHF 20,000 minimum share capital.
