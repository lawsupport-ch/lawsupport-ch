Switzerland separates economic freedom from regulatory oversight with unusual clarity. The Federal Constitution guarantees the right to conduct business (Art. 27 BV), and most commercial activities need nothing more than a Commercial Register entry. But where public safety, financial stability or market integrity is at stake, Swiss regulators — FINMA, Swissmedic, SECO, and cantonal authorities — impose licensing requirements that are rigorous, specific, and enforced. This guide covers the three main licensing domains: financial market regulation through FINMA, the fast-evolving crypto and fintech framework, and industry-specific permits for sectors from healthcare to gold trading.

## Swiss Regulatory Framework

Swiss business regulation operates on a principle of minimal interference with maximum enforcement. The state does not require a general business licence — you register your company and begin trading. But once you enter a regulated sector, the requirements are exacting and the penalties for non-compliance are severe.

### The Three Regulatory Tiers

**Federal regulators** oversee sectors where uniform national standards are essential:

- <a href="https://www.finma.ch/en/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">FINMA</a> — banks, insurers, asset managers, securities firms, payment systems
- <a href="https://www.swissmedic.ch/swissmedic/en/home.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Swissmedic</a> — pharmaceuticals, medical devices, clinical trials
- <a href="https://www.seco.admin.ch/seco/en/home.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">SECO</a> — export controls, dual-use goods, sanctions enforcement
- BAZL — civil aviation
- BAV — railway operations

**Cantonal authorities** regulate activities where local conditions matter:

- Hospitality and food service (Wirtepatent)
- Healthcare practitioners and facilities
- Private security firms
- Construction and environmental permits
- Education and childcare institutions

**Self-Regulatory Organisations (SROs)** fill the space between full federal supervision and no regulation at all. SROs supervise financial intermediaries who are not directly regulated by FINMA — including crypto businesses, loan brokers, payment processors, and precious metals dealers. SRO membership carries AML compliance obligations under the <a href="https://www.fedlex.admin.ch/eli/cc/1998/892_892_892/en" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA)</a>.

### Licensing vs Registration

An important distinction runs through Swiss regulatory law: some activities require a licence (Bewilligung), whilst others require only registration (Registrierung) or notification (Meldung). The difference is material.

A **licence** means you cannot begin the activity until the authority has reviewed your application and granted approval. Operating without it is illegal.

A **registration** means you notify the authority that you are conducting the activity. The authority records your registration and may impose conditions, but you can begin operations upon registration.

A **notification** is lighter still — you inform the authority, and they acknowledge receipt. No approval is needed.

Understanding which category your business falls into is the first step. Getting it wrong — assuming registration when a licence is required — can result in enforcement action, fines, and forced cessation of business.

### Why Switzerland Attracts Regulated Businesses

Despite demanding requirements, Switzerland remains one of the most attractive jurisdictions for regulated businesses. Several factors drive this:

- **Legal certainty**: rules are published, stable, and applied consistently
- **Regulatory quality**: FINMA is regarded as one of the world's most professional financial regulators
- **Political stability**: no sudden regulatory shifts driven by electoral cycles
- **Tax competitiveness**: corporate tax rates of 11–15% depending on canton
- **Talent pool**: multilingual, highly educated workforce with strong financial services expertise
- **International recognition**: Swiss licences carry credibility with counterparties and investors globally

The cost of obtaining a Swiss licence is higher than in many jurisdictions. The value of holding one is correspondingly greater.

## FINMA: Financial Market Licensing

FINMA is the centrepiece of Swiss financial regulation. It authorises and supervises all entities that accept deposits, manage assets, issue securities, provide insurance, or operate payment systems. The legal framework spans multiple federal acts, each governing a specific category of financial institution.

Our detailed guide to [FINMA licensing](/licensing/finma-licensing-guide/) covers every licence category, from banking to asset management to FinSA obligations. Here is the synthesis.

### Licence Categories

**Banking licence (BankG)**: Required for entities that accept public deposits and engage in lending. Minimum capital: CHF 10 million (in practice, CHF 20–50 million for a viable operation). Requirements include dual control (Vier-Augen-Prinzip), fit and proper directors and shareholders, a FINMA-approved auditor, and documented AML procedures. Timeline: 12–24 months.

**Fintech licence (Art. 1b BankG)**: A lighter alternative for entities that accept public deposits up to CHF 100 million without investing them. Minimum capital: CHF 300,000. Suited for payment platforms and e-money operators. Timeline: 6–12 months.

**Securities firm licence (FINIG)**: Required for entities that trade securities for client accounts, underwrite issues, or operate as market makers. Capital requirements vary by activity. Timeline: 6–12 months.

**Asset manager licence (FINIG)**: Required for portfolio managers and trustees of collective investment schemes. Minimum capital: CHF 100,000 or one-quarter of fixed costs (whichever is higher). Requires SRO membership or direct FINMA supervision. Timeline: 3–9 months.

**Insurance licence (VAG)**: Required for entities underwriting insurance or reinsurance contracts. Capital requirements are risk-based and can run to hundreds of millions of CHF for large insurers. Timeline: 12–18 months.

**FinSA obligations**: The Financial Services Act (FIDLEG) imposes conduct rules on all financial service providers, including suitability assessments, prospectus requirements, and client documentation obligations. FinSA does not create a separate licence category but adds compliance layers to all existing FINMA-licensed activities.

### Pre-Application Discussions

FINMA encourages applicants to engage in informal pre-application discussions before filing a formal application. These discussions — which are confidential and non-binding — allow FINMA to flag potential issues early, clarify requirements specific to your business model, and give you a realistic assessment of the timeline.

Skipping the pre-application stage is one of the most common and costly mistakes. Applicants who file directly often receive extensive supplementary information requests, which restart the review clock and can add months to the process.

### The Fit and Proper Test

Every FINMA licence application includes a fit and proper assessment of directors, senior management, and qualifying shareholders (those holding 10% or more). FINMA examines:

- Professional qualifications and experience
- Regulatory history (sanctions, enforcement actions, bans)
- Criminal record (financial crimes, fraud, AML violations)
- Financial soundness (personal bankruptcies, debt enforcement proceedings)
- Conflicts of interest

A single individual who fails the fit and proper test can block the entire application. This is the most common reason for applications stalling or failing. Address governance issues before filing — not after FINMA raises them.

## Crypto and Fintech Licensing

Switzerland positioned itself early as a crypto-friendly jurisdiction, and the regulatory framework has matured accordingly. The DLT Act (2021) introduced specific legal categories for blockchain-based financial instruments and trading platforms, building on FINMA's existing guidance from the 2018 ICO guidelines.

Our detailed guide to [crypto and fintech licensing](/licensing/crypto-fintech-licensing-switzerland/) covers VASP registration, SRO membership, DLT trading facilities, ICO rules, and the fintech sandbox. Here is the synthesis.

### The Swiss Crypto Regulatory Stack

Swiss crypto regulation is layered, not monolithic. Different activities trigger different requirements:

**Level 1 — No licence required**: Developing blockchain software, providing technical infrastructure, or offering non-custodial wallet services where users retain control of their private keys. These activities do not constitute financial intermediation under Swiss law.

**Level 2 — SRO membership**: Exchanging cryptocurrencies for fiat or other crypto (exchange services), custodying crypto assets for clients, facilitating crypto payments, or brokering crypto transactions. These activities qualify as financial intermediation under the AMLA, requiring SRO membership for AML compliance. The main SROs accepting crypto businesses are VQF and SO-FIT.

**Level 3 — FINMA authorisation**: Operating a crypto trading platform (DLT trading facility licence), issuing tokens classified as securities (prospectus requirements), managing crypto investment funds (asset manager licence), or providing crypto-based banking services (banking or fintech licence).

### DLT Trading Facilities

The DLT Act created a new FINMA licence category: the DLT trading facility. This is a regulated marketplace for trading tokenised securities (DLT securities as defined in the Swiss Code of Obligations, Art. 973d et seq.). Unlike traditional exchanges, DLT trading facilities can admit retail participants directly, provide custody services, and handle clearing and settlement on-chain.

The licence requires:

- Adequate organisation, qualified management, risk management framework
- Compliance with AML rules
- Participant protection measures (segregation of assets, insolvency protection)
- Operational resilience (cyber security, business continuity)

As of 2026, only a handful of DLT trading facility licences have been granted, reflecting both the novelty of the licence category and the rigour of FINMA's assessment.

### ICO and Token Classification

FINMA classifies tokens into three categories, each with different regulatory consequences:

- **Payment tokens** (cryptocurrencies): qualify as financial intermediation if exchanged or custodied for others — SRO membership required
- **Utility tokens**: not financial instruments if they provide access to a service and are fully functional at issuance — minimal regulation
- **Asset tokens** (security tokens): treated as securities — prospectus requirements, securities dealer obligations, and potentially exchange listing rules apply

Many tokens have hybrid characteristics (e.g., a utility token with a profit-sharing component). FINMA assesses the economic substance, not the label. An ICO/ITO issuer should obtain a FINMA no-action letter confirming the classification before proceeding — this is standard practice in Switzerland and costs CHF 5,000 to 10,000 in regulatory fees.

### The Innovation Sandbox

Swiss law provides a regulatory sandbox for small-scale financial innovation. Entities that accept deposits of less than CHF 1 million from the public, provided the deposits are not invested, can operate without any FINMA licence or SRO membership. This sandbox allows startups to test business models with real users before committing to the cost and time of formal licensing.

The sandbox is not an exemption from AML rules — if the activity constitutes financial intermediation, AML compliance is still required regardless of the deposit threshold.

### Crypto Valley Zug

The canton of Zug has earned its "Crypto Valley" reputation through a combination of favourable tax rates, a concentrated ecosystem of blockchain companies, supportive cantonal authorities, and proximity to Zurich's financial infrastructure. More than 1,000 blockchain-related companies are registered in Zug. The cantonal commercial register office has experience processing crypto company incorporations, and local banks (notably Seba Bank and Sygnum, both FINMA-licensed) provide banking services specifically for crypto businesses.

For all details on crypto licensing pathways, SRO membership, DLT trading facilities, and ICO guidance, see our [crypto and fintech licensing guide](/licensing/crypto-fintech-licensing-switzerland/).

## Industry-Specific Licences

Beyond financial regulation, Switzerland requires activity-specific licences in sectors where public health, safety, or strategic interests are at stake. These licences are issued by a mix of federal and cantonal authorities, with requirements that can vary significantly by location.

Our detailed guide to [industry-specific licences](/licensing/industry-licences-switzerland/) covers trade permits, healthcare authorisations, export controls, gold trading, and hospitality licensing. Here is the synthesis.

### Hospitality and Food Service

Operating a restaurant, bar, hotel, or food production facility in Switzerland requires a cantonal hospitality licence — typically called a Wirtepatent (German-speaking cantons) or patente d'aubergiste (French-speaking cantons). Requirements include:

- Completion of a cantonal hospitality course (2–4 weeks)
- Passing a written and/or oral examination
- Premises meeting fire safety, hygiene, and accessibility standards
- Good character certificate (Leumundszeugnis)

The licence is personal — it belongs to the licence holder, not the premises. If the licence holder leaves, a new holder must be appointed. Some cantons offer provisional licences while the application is processed, allowing the business to open before the full licence is issued.

Cost: CHF 2,000–5,000 including course fees. Timeline: 4–12 weeks after course completion.

### Healthcare

Healthcare regulation is split between Swissmedic (federal) and cantonal health departments. The division is functional:

- **Swissmedic**: authorises pharmaceutical manufacturers, approves medicinal products for the Swiss market, regulates medical devices, and oversees clinical trials
- **Cantonal health departments**: licence healthcare practitioners (doctors, dentists, nurses, physiotherapists), hospitals, clinics, pharmacies, and laboratories

Medical practitioners who qualified abroad must have their qualifications recognised by <a href="https://www.bag.admin.ch/bag/en/home.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">MEBEKO</a> (the Swiss medical professions commission) before applying for a cantonal practice authorisation. EU/EFTA qualifications benefit from mutual recognition; non-EU qualifications face a more extensive verification process.

The new Swiss Medical Devices Ordinance (MepV), aligned with EU MDR, imposes additional requirements on medical device manufacturers and distributors, including conformity assessments, post-market surveillance, and Unique Device Identification (UDI).

### Export Controls

The <a href="https://www.seco.admin.ch/seco/en/home/Aussenwirtschaftspolitik/Wirtschaftliche_Landesversorgung/Exportkontrollen.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">State Secretariat for Economic Affairs (SECO)</a> administers export controls on:

- Dual-use goods (items with both civilian and military applications)
- Military equipment and weapons
- Goods subject to international sanctions (UN, EU-aligned)
- Nuclear materials and technology

Exporters of controlled goods must obtain SECO export licences before shipping. The Goods Control Act (GKG) and its ordinances define the controlled items list, which aligns closely with EU and Wassenaar Arrangement lists. Individual export licences are issued per shipment; general export licences cover repeated shipments of the same goods to the same destination.

Violations carry criminal penalties including fines and imprisonment of up to 10 years. Internal compliance programmes (ICP) are strongly recommended for any company dealing in potentially controlled goods.

### Gold and Precious Metals

Switzerland is the world's largest gold refining hub, processing roughly 70% of the world's gold. Gold trading triggers several regulatory requirements:

- **SRO membership**: precious metals dealing is classified as financial intermediation under the AMLA, requiring AML compliance through an SRO
- **Precious Metals Control Act**: applies to refiners and assayers — hallmarking, purity certification, and Swiss assay office supervision
- **Customs declarations**: gold imports/exports above CHF 100,000 require customs declaration
- **Responsible sourcing**: LBMA Good Delivery refiners must comply with responsible gold guidance; Swiss refineries face increasing due diligence requirements on supply chains

SRO membership for gold trading: CHF 20,000–50,000 in the first year. Ongoing costs depend on transaction volume.

For all details on non-financial licences, see our [industry-specific licences guide](/licensing/industry-licences-switzerland/).

## The Application Process

Whilst every licence has its own specific requirements, the general application process follows a common pattern across Swiss regulators.

### Step 1 — Preliminary Assessment

Before investing in a formal application, determine which licences your business actually needs. This is not always obvious. A business that exchanges crypto for fiat needs SRO membership. A business that also custodies crypto for clients may additionally need FINMA authorisation. A business that issues tokens may trigger securities regulations. Getting the classification right at the outset prevents expensive course corrections later.

### Step 2 — Pre-Application Engagement

For FINMA-regulated activities, request a pre-application meeting. FINMA staff will review your business model, flag potential issues, and indicate what the formal application should contain. For cantonal licences, contact the relevant cantonal office for guidance on their specific requirements and documentation.

### Step 3 — Documentation

Prepare the application dossier. Common requirements across all licence types include:

- Legal entity documentation (articles of association, Commercial Register extract)
- Business plan with financial projections
- Organisational chart with fit and proper documentation for key persons
- Compliance framework (AML, risk management, internal controls)
- IT and operational resilience documentation
- Proof of capital adequacy

### Step 4 — Formal Filing

Submit the complete dossier. FINMA and Swissmedic both emphasise that incomplete applications are not processed — they are returned with a request for supplementary information, and the review clock does not start until the dossier is complete.

### Step 5 — Review and Queries

The regulatory authority reviews the application, typically raising supplementary questions. Response times to these queries directly affect the total timeline. Slow responses are the single largest controllable factor in application delays.

### Step 6 — Decision

The authority grants the licence (possibly with conditions), requests amendments, or rejects the application. Conditional licences are common — FINMA may grant a conditional banking licence that becomes unconditional once final capital is paid in and the FINMA-approved auditor confirms operational readiness.

### Step 7 — Post-Licence Obligations

Holding a licence is not a one-time event. Ongoing obligations include periodic reporting, regulatory audits, notification of material changes, and compliance with evolving regulations. Budget for ongoing compliance from the outset.

## Common Regulatory Mistakes

Experience with Swiss licensing applications reveals several patterns that delay approvals or lead to rejection.

**Underestimating preparation time.** The formal review period is only part of the timeline. Preparation — structuring the entity, drafting the compliance framework, assembling the governance team, engaging auditors — often takes as long as the regulatory review itself. Applicants who rush to file before the dossier is complete face supplementary information requests that add months.

**Governance gaps.** FINMA's fit and proper assessment catches applicants off guard. A director with a regulatory sanction in another jurisdiction, a shareholder structure that FINMA cannot look through to ultimate beneficial owners, or a compliance officer without relevant experience — any of these can stall the application. Vet your governance team before filing.

**Wrong licence category.** Applying for a licence you do not need (or failing to apply for one you do) wastes time and money. A crypto business that applies for a full banking licence when SRO membership would suffice overspends by an order of magnitude. A business that assumes SRO membership is sufficient when FINMA authorisation is actually required faces enforcement action.

**Ignoring cantonal requirements.** Federal licences (FINMA, Swissmedic) do not exempt you from cantonal requirements. A FINMA-licensed bank still needs cantonal building permits for its offices, cantonal work permits for its employees, and potentially municipal approvals for signage and operations.

**Treating compliance as a cost centre.** Businesses that invest minimally in compliance during the application phase face problems post-licensing. FINMA's first regulatory audit typically occurs within 12 months of licence issuance. Arriving at that audit with incomplete policies, untrained staff, or undocumented procedures triggers enforcement measures — conditions, formal warnings, or in severe cases, licence revocation.

**Not engaging local counsel.** Foreign applicants sometimes attempt to navigate Swiss licensing from abroad, using home-jurisdiction advisers unfamiliar with Swiss regulatory practice. Swiss regulators communicate in German, French, or Italian (not English as a first language), follow Swiss administrative procedures, and expect familiarity with Swiss legal concepts. Local counsel with direct regulatory experience materially improves outcomes.

## Maintaining Your Licence

A licence is not a certificate you frame and forget. Swiss regulators actively supervise licensed entities through a combination of periodic reporting, regulatory audits, on-site inspections, and ongoing dialogue.

### Reporting Obligations

FINMA-supervised entities submit quarterly and annual reports covering financial position, capital adequacy, risk exposures, and AML activity. Swissmedic-regulated manufacturers report adverse events, quality deviations, and supply chain changes. SECO licence holders report on controlled goods exports.

Late or inaccurate reporting triggers regulatory attention. Persistent reporting failures can lead to formal enforcement proceedings.

### Regulatory Audits

FINMA-licensed entities are audited annually by a FINMA-approved auditor (pruefgesellschaft). The audit covers financial statements and regulatory compliance. The auditor reports directly to FINMA, not to the entity's management. Audit findings that indicate material compliance gaps require remediation within prescribed timeframes.

### Material Changes

Licensed entities must notify the regulatory authority of material changes before they occur. Material changes include:

- Changes in qualifying shareholders (10%+ ownership)
- Appointments or departures of directors and senior management
- Significant business model changes
- Changes in the compliance framework
- Cross-border expansion or new activities

Failing to notify material changes is itself a regulatory violation, regardless of whether the change would have been approved.

### Licence Renewal and Revocation

Most Swiss licences are issued indefinitely, subject to ongoing compliance. FINMA does not impose fixed renewal periods for banking or asset management licences. However, FINMA can revoke a licence if the entity no longer meets the conditions under which it was granted — capital falls below minimum, governance deteriorates, or serious compliance failures are identified.

Cantonal licences (hospitality, healthcare) may have fixed terms requiring periodic renewal. Check the specific conditions attached to your licence.
