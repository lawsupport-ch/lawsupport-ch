Choosing the best Swiss bank for foreigners is not a matter of picking the most recognisable name. It is a matter of matching your business profile — your industry, your ownership structure, your nationality, your transaction volumes — to the bank that currently has the compliance appetite and operational infrastructure to accept you.

Apply to the wrong bank and you will wait four to eight weeks for a rejection. Apply to the right bank and you can have a fully functional CHF account within a similar timeframe. This guide is built on 18 years of opening Swiss bank accounts for foreign-owned companies registered across 40+ nationalities. We tell you what actually works in 2026, not what the bank's brochure says.

If you are looking for a foundational overview of the process, start with our [Swiss bank account guide](/banking/swiss-bank-account/). For the specific documentation requirements, see our [corporate bank account Switzerland page](/banking/corporate-bank-account-switzerland/).

## Why Bank Selection Matters More Than You Think

Switzerland has over 200 licensed banks, as listed in the <a href="https://www.finma.ch/en/authorisation/finma-register/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">FINMA Register of authorised institutions</a>. They are not interchangeable. Each institution sets its own compliance policy, its own sector appetite, and its own threshold for what constitutes an acceptable foreign relationship. These policies change. A cantonal bank that was welcoming to non-EU founders two years ago may have tightened its KYC framework significantly since then. A bank that refused crypto businesses in 2021 may have built a dedicated digital assets desk by 2025.

The most expensive mistake a foreign business owner makes is submitting a cold application to a bank that was never going to accept their profile. You lose time. You may generate a rejection on record. And in some cases, a refusal from one Swiss institution makes the next application harder.

The right approach — and the approach we use at Morgan Hartley Consulting — is to profile the business first, then identify which institutions are currently open to that profile, then approach with a complete and well-prepared application package.

## The Swiss Banking System: A Practical Overview

For a deeper look at the overall structure of Swiss banking regulation, see our [Swiss banking system guide](/banking/swiss-banking-system/).

### Big Banks

**UBS** is now Switzerland's dominant universal bank following its absorption of Credit Suisse. It operates at scale, with FATCA-compliant infrastructure, multi-currency platforms, and the institutional capacity to handle complex ownership structures. UBS is not the easiest entry point for small foreign companies — its compliance processes are thorough and relationship-dependent — but for the right profile it is a strong option, particularly for US-person-owned entities and larger holding structures.

### Cantonal Banks

Switzerland has 24 cantons, and most have their own cantonal bank. The major ones relevant to foreign-owned businesses include:

- **ZKB (Zürcher Kantonalbank)** — Zurich's cantonal bank, the largest in the system, with significant capacity for business accounts and reasonable experience with EU/EFTA-national clients.
- **BCG (Banque Cantonale de Genève)** — Geneva's cantonal bank, a logical first contact for French-speaking Switzerland and internationally active businesses registered in Geneva.
- **BCV (Banque Cantonale Vaudoise)** — Vaud canton's bank, covering Lausanne and the surrounding region.
- **Graubündner Kantonalbank** — A smaller institution with a reputation for pragmatic relationship banking, including for some foreign structures.
- **Zuger Kantonalbank** — The primary bank for Zug-based companies. Zuger Kantonalbank operates with lower asset thresholds than UBS and is more accessible for smaller international structures. It is accustomed to foreign shareholders, multi-currency accounts, and holding company banking. For companies registered in Zug, this is typically the most straightforward first application.
- **Schwyzer Kantonalbank** — Relevant for companies incorporated in Schwyz, another common canton for international holding structures.

Cantonal banks are relationship-driven institutions. They serve the economic interests of their canton. A company incorporated in Zug with genuine economic substance in Zug — a real office, a real director, real operational activity — has a reasonable basis for approaching the local cantonal bank. A letterbox company with no local ties will struggle.

### Private and Wealth Banks

**Pictet**, **Lombard Odier**, **Julius Baer**, and **EFG International** operate in a different tier. These are relationship banks serving high-net-worth individuals and family offices. They are not retail product providers. The real thresholds (not the published minimums): UBS and Julius Baer require CHF 500’000+ under management for non-resident corporate clients. Pictet and Lombard Odier typically require CHF 4–6 million in investable assets to initiate a private banking relationship — these are not negotiable. Julius Baer can also serve as a banking counterparty for larger corporate structures with significant shareholders. For a full breakdown of the private banking tier, see our [private banking Switzerland guide](/banking/private-banking-switzerland/).

### Neo-Banking and Fintech

**Relio AG** (Zurich-based fintech, CHF 249/month) is a licensed provider offering corporate accounts with same-day onboarding after video verification through Intrum. For European-owned operating companies, Relio is currently the fastest route to a functional Swiss corporate account. The hard limitation: Relio explicitly rejects companies with any US nexus at the UBO or shareholder level.

**Wise Business**, **Revolut Business**, and **Neon Business** are not Swiss banks in the traditional regulatory sense, though they can hold CHF balances and execute Swiss payment instructions. They are fast to set up — sometimes within days — and useful for businesses in early-stage operations or those needing multi-currency functionality without a full banking relationship.

The tradeoff is significant: no credit facilities, lower counterparty credibility in traditional Swiss business dealings, and in some sectors, a Wise or Revolut account will not satisfy the requirements of Swiss landlords, notaries, or institutional partners who expect a relationship with a licensed Swiss bank.

### Crypto-Specialist Banks

**SEBA Bank** (rebranded as AMINA Bank) and **Sygnum Bank** are the two FINMA-licensed banking institutions purpose-built for digital asset businesses. They accept crypto-native companies, blockchain infrastructure firms, and token issuers that cannot access conventional Swiss banking. These are not workaround solutions — they are licensed, regulated institutions with full banking rights. However, they have their own qualification criteria and the application process is rigorous.

## Which Swiss Bank Is Right for Your Business?

### Standard Operating Company (Trading, Services, Tech) — EU/EFTA Owner

**Best fit:** Cantonal bank in the company's canton of incorporation.

If your company is incorporated in Zurich, approach ZKB. If in Geneva, BCG. If in Zug — the most common jurisdiction for international structures — Zuger Kantonalbank or Schwyzer Kantonalbank are the first contacts, with ZKB as a secondary option given its scale and international client experience.

EU and EEA nationals generally face the most straightforward KYC process at cantonal banks. Switzerland and the EU share AML frameworks that cantonal compliance teams understand well. The bank wants to see clean corporate documentation, a clear business purpose, and evidence of real economic activity.

**Expected timeline:** 4–8 weeks from submission of complete application.

---

### Holding Company with Foreign Shareholders

**Best fit:** ZKB for mid-sized structures; UBS or Julius Baer for larger or more complex structures.

Holding companies raise additional questions around beneficial ownership, dividend flows, and the source of funds into the holding vehicle. Transparency of the ownership structure is not optional — it is the foundation of the application. Incomplete or unclear UBO (Ultimate Beneficial Owner) documentation is the single most common reason holding company applications stall or fail.

For holding companies with shareholders from higher-scrutiny jurisdictions, larger banks with dedicated KYC teams are more appropriate than smaller cantonal institutions.

**Expected timeline:** 6–10 weeks, depending on ownership complexity.

---

### Crypto and Blockchain Business

**Best fit:** SEBA/AMINA Bank or Sygnum Bank — exclusively.

Standard Swiss banks, including cantonal banks, do not provide primary banking services to crypto-native businesses. This is not a negotiating point. The compliance infrastructure required to handle virtual asset transactions, token issuance, or crypto-to-fiat flows does not exist at most conventional Swiss institutions.

AMINA and Sygnum are built for exactly this client profile. If your business is classified as a VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider) under FINMA's framework, you will need to complete VASP registration before or in parallel with your banking application. Both banks will want to understand your technology stack, your AML controls, your token classification (if applicable), and your target counterparties.

**Expected timeline:** 6–12 weeks. Do not begin this process without a fully developed compliance framework already in place.

---

### US-Person-Owned Company

**Best fit:** UBS, or cantonal banks with documented FATCA compliance programmes.

The US Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act requires Swiss banks to report account data for US persons to the IRS. Many smaller cantonal banks have made a deliberate business decision not to invest in FATCA reporting infrastructure, which means they have also made a decision not to serve US-person clients. Presenting as a US citizen or green card holder to an unprepared institution wastes everyone's time.

UBS has FATCA compliance at institutional scale. Some of the larger cantonal banks — ZKB among them — have also invested in this capacity.

The practical reality is harsher than the regulatory framework suggests. Most Swiss banks and fintechs — including Relio — will not onboard companies with a US person at the UBO, shareholder, or director level. One US-owned Swiss AG we worked with was rejected by Relio with the statement that they are “unable to onboard companies with US nexus at UBO/shareholders level.” The company was forced to route payments through a UK parent entity’s account with Ebury (a payment service, not a proper bank account), which created serious compliance issues. Swiss regulators take a dim view of companies operating through another entity’s bank account.

The only reliable path for US-person-owned Swiss companies is UBS (with the CHF 500’000+ threshold) or a cantonal bank with documented FATCA infrastructure. Budget additional time and prepare for at least one rejection. For a more detailed breakdown, see our dedicated page on [opening a Swiss bank account as a US person](/banking/open-swiss-bank-account-us/).

**Expected timeline:** 6–10 weeks with a prepared FATCA-ready application, assuming you apply to the right institution first.

---

### High-Net-Worth Personal Banking

**Best fit:** Pictet, Lombard Odier, Julius Baer, or EFG International.

Swiss private banking is a relationship business, not a product business. There is no online application form. Entry typically requires a formal introduction, a first meeting with a relationship manager, and investable assets above CHF 500,000 — with some institutions setting their practical threshold at CHF 1 million or higher. These banks manage your wealth; they do not provide a CHF current account in isolation.

---

### Small Company Needing Fast Setup

**Best fit:** Wise Business, Revolut Business, or Neon Business.

If your priority is speed — getting a CHF IBAN and the ability to send and receive payments within days — then fintech providers are the fastest route. This is a legitimate solution for early-stage companies, for bridging periods while a full banking relationship is being established, or for companies with straightforward multi-currency needs and no requirement for credit facilities.

Understand the limitations clearly: these are not Swiss bank accounts in the regulatory sense, they will not satisfy all Swiss counterparties, and they provide no path to a credit facility or lending relationship.

## What to Look for When Choosing a Swiss Bank

Beyond the broad match between business profile and bank type, four specific factors should drive your selection:

**1. Industry appetite.** Does the bank currently accept clients in your sector? Sectors that regularly face heightened scrutiny include financial services, legal and compliance consultancy, real estate, commodities trading, and any business touching digital assets. A bank's published policy and its operational practice are not always the same. Knowing which sectors a bank's compliance team is comfortable with — right now, not two years ago — is a material advantage.

**2. Nationality experience.** Swiss KYC requirements apply to everyone, but the depth and complexity of the due diligence process varies significantly by passport country. A Swiss bank that regularly serves clients from India, the UAE, or Southeast Asia has developed processes for navigating the documentation chains required. A bank with no experience serving a particular nationality will create delays and friction even for a well-qualified applicant.

**3. Digital banking capability.** Evaluate the bank's e-banking platform, API access for accounting software integration, multi-currency account availability, and mobile banking functionality. Swiss banking infrastructure varies significantly — some institutions have invested heavily in digital platforms; others remain operationally traditional.

**4. Fees and FX.** CHF account maintenance fees, transaction costs, and foreign exchange spreads differ considerably across Swiss institutions. For an operating company making regular international payments, these differences compound materially over time.

**5. Credit facilities.** If your business may require an overdraft facility, a business loan, or trade finance, ensure the bank you select can provide this. Neo-banking providers cannot. Some smaller cantonal banks operate with conservative lending policies for foreign-owned entities.

## Warning Signs That an Application Will Be Rejected

Not every foreign company is bankable at a Swiss institution today. The warning signs that a cold application is likely to fail include: an unclear or circular ownership structure; a business activity that is inherently high-risk under FINMA guidelines without the corresponding compliance infrastructure; principals from jurisdictions on FATF grey or black lists; a company with no genuine Swiss economic connection seeking an account purely for financial convenience; and prior rejections from Swiss banks that have not been addressed at a structural level.

The <a href="https://www.snb.ch/en/the-snb/mandates-goals/financial-stability" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Swiss National Bank's financial stability framework</a> sets the broader regulatory environment within which all Swiss banks operate. Understanding that framework helps explain why banks apply the criteria they do.

For a full breakdown of the most common reasons Swiss bank account applications fail, and how to address them before applying, see our [corporate bank account Switzerland guide](/banking/corporate-bank-account-switzerland/) and our [Swiss bank account for non-residents page](/banking/swiss-bank-account-non-residents/).

## Hidden Requirements and Compliance Traps

Swiss bank applications fail for reasons that are not documented on any bank's website. These are patterns from our case files:

**PostFinance's undocumented requirements:** Beyond standard KYC, PostFinance requires CVs of all directors, copies of employment contracts, detailed transaction descriptions (what payments will flow through the account, from whom, and in what currencies), and a written business plan. They rejected a company called "BK Tesla AI Academy" over the brand-sensitivity of the word "Tesla." Two other companies — one in health services (Edel Swiss Health), one in pharmaceuticals (Aquavit Pharmaceuticals) — were rejected with clean documentation and no apparent risk factors.

**The c/o address penalty:** Companies registered at a c/o address (care-of a formation agent) face higher scrutiny than those with a direct street address. Banks assess substance partly through address quality. If you can secure a direct registered address, do so before applying.

**Tax filing as proof of substance:** Without submitted Swiss tax filings, a company cannot demonstrate genuine local operation to a bank's compliance team. Newly formed companies should file their first tax return as soon as possible — it becomes a credibility document for banking.

**The blocked account signal:** If a Swiss bank approves your [capital deposit account](/banking/capital-deposit-account-switzerland/) during company formation, there is roughly an 80% probability it will also approve the permanent operating account. This is the strongest predictive signal in the Swiss banking process.

**US person trap:** A US-owned Swiss employer-of-record company with three Swiss employees was rejected by Relio (hard US-nexus policy), then by PostFinance. It was forced to use a UK parent entity's payment account — creating ongoing compliance issues because Swiss regulators view operations through another entity's account as questionable.

**Can a non-resident foreigner open a Swiss bank account for their company?**

Yes. Swiss law does not prohibit non-residents from holding accounts at Swiss banks, and Swiss banks do serve foreign-owned companies. However, each bank sets its own eligibility criteria. Non-residency increases the depth of due diligence required, particularly around source of funds and the commercial rationale for banking in Switzerland. Our [Swiss bank account for non-residents](/banking/swiss-bank-account-non-residents/) page covers this in detail.

**How long does it take to open a Swiss business bank account as a foreigner?**

For a well-prepared application at an appropriate institution, most business accounts open within four to eight weeks of submission. Holding companies, crypto-related businesses, and structures with complex ownership can take ten to twelve weeks. Fintech solutions such as Wise Business can be operational within a few days, though they are not equivalent to a Swiss banking relationship.

**Which Swiss bank is easiest for foreigners to open an account with?**

There is no universal answer because ease of access depends entirely on your profile. A Zurich-based tech company owned by a German national will find a cantonal bank application relatively straightforward. A holding company with shareholders from multiple non-EU jurisdictions will have a more complex path regardless of which bank it approaches. The right bank is the one matched to your profile, not the one with the fewest requirements in the abstract.

**Do Swiss banks accept US citizens?**

Yes, but not all of them. FATCA compliance is a prerequisite, and many smaller Swiss banks have chosen not to maintain the reporting infrastructure required to serve US persons. UBS and certain larger cantonal banks do accept US-person clients with the appropriate documentation. See our dedicated guide on [opening a Swiss bank account as a US person](/banking/open-swiss-bank-account-us/) for a full breakdown.

**What is the minimum deposit required to open a Swiss business bank account?**

Cantonal banks and UBS typically do not require a minimum deposit for business current accounts, though they scrutinise expected transaction volumes and business activity. Private wealth banks require substantial investable assets — typically CHF 500,000 to CHF 1 million or more — as a condition of the relationship. Fintech providers such as Wise and Revolut have no meaningful minimum balance requirement.

**What KYC documents do Swiss banks require from foreigners?**

The core KYC package for a foreign-owned company typically includes: certified copies of the commercial register extract, articles of association, and shareholder register; certified passports and proof of address for all UBOs holding more than 25%; a source-of-funds declaration; a business description with expected transaction volumes; and, for complex structures, a group chart showing the full ownership chain. Banks may request additional documents depending on the applicant's nationality, industry, and jurisdiction of incorporation.

**Can I open a Swiss bank account online as a foreigner?**

Full online account opening for foreign-owned companies is not available at most Swiss banks. Cantonal banks and UBS require in-person identity verification or certified document submission at minimum. Some fintech providers (Wise Business, Revolut Business) offer fully remote onboarding, but these are not licensed Swiss banks and carry the limitations described in this guide.

**How do fees compare across Swiss banks for foreign businesses?**

Fee structures vary significantly. Cantonal banks typically charge monthly account maintenance fees between CHF 20 and CHF 80 for business accounts, plus per-transaction fees for domestic and international payments. UBS fees are broadly similar but may include relationship management costs for larger accounts. Private banks charge based on assets under management rather than transaction volume. Fintech providers tend to be cheaper for international transfers but charge currency conversion fees.

**What is the rejection rate for foreign companies applying to Swiss banks?**

There is no published industry figure, but in practice the majority of cold applications from foreign-owned companies are unsuccessful. From our case files, the most common rejection triggers are: (1) US person at any level of the ownership chain, (2) brand-sensitive company names that trigger compliance questions, (3) insufficient documentation of business model and revenue source, (4) AML/WorldCheck flags on directors or UBOs, (5) UBOs from high-risk or sanctioned jurisdictions, and (6) no existing Swiss banking relationship. Applications prepared with professional guidance and directed to the appropriate institution have a substantially higher success rate.

**Can foreigners open multi-currency accounts at Swiss banks?**

Yes. UBS and the larger cantonal banks offer multi-currency account facilities, typically covering EUR, USD, GBP, and CHF as standard with additional currencies available on request. Private banks routinely manage multi-currency portfolios. Fintech providers such as Wise Business offer the broadest currency coverage, though without the banking relationship that traditional institutions provide.

**What is the difference between cantonal banks and UBS for foreign companies?**

Cantonal banks are regionally focused, relationship-driven institutions that serve the economic interests of their canton. They suit companies with genuine local substance and EU/EFTA ownership. UBS operates at national and global scale, with more developed infrastructure for complex ownership structures, FATCA compliance, and clients from a wider range of nationalities. For straightforward structures, cantonal banks are often the faster and more practical route. For complex or higher-risk profiles, UBS's dedicated KYC resources are an advantage.

**Does Swiss bank secrecy still apply to foreign account holders?**

Swiss banking secrecy as it existed before 2017 no longer functions as a tool for tax concealment. Switzerland participates in the OECD's Common Reporting Standard (CRS) and exchanges financial account information automatically with over 100 partner jurisdictions. FATCA reporting applies to US persons. What remains is a strong domestic legal framework protecting client confidentiality from private parties — but foreign tax authorities with an applicable treaty or CRS arrangement can and do receive account data. The <a href="https://www.admin.ch/opc/en/classified-compilation/19340083/index.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Swiss Banking Act (BA)</a> governs the current legal position on client data and confidentiality.
