Swiss VAT (Mehrwertsteuer, MWST) registration is mandatory once a business's worldwide taxable turnover exceeds CHF 100,000 per year. The standard rate is 8.1% since January 2024. This guide covers who must register, the registration process, VAT rates, foreign company obligations, and the most common compliance mistakes.

---

## Who Must Register for Swiss VAT

Under the <a href="https://www.fedlex.admin.ch/eli/cc/2009/615/en" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Swiss VAT Act (MWSTG)</a>, registration is mandatory for any business that:

- Generates CHF 100,000 or more in taxable worldwide turnover per calendar year, and
- Is not exempt from VAT liability under special provisions

The CHF 100,000 threshold applies to gross taxable turnover globally — not only Swiss-sourced revenue. A Swiss GmbH that generates CHF 90,000 in Switzerland and CHF 30,000 abroad must register, because total worldwide taxable turnover exceeds the threshold.

**Exempt from mandatory registration:**

- Agricultural and forestry businesses with turnover below CHF 150,000 (special exemption)
- Non-profit sports and cultural associations with turnover below CHF 250,000
- Businesses that supply only exempt-without-credit (steuerausgenommene) services, such as certain educational, medical, or financial services

**Start date for new businesses:**

A newly registered company is not obligated to register for VAT at incorporation. Registration becomes mandatory once the CHF 100,000 threshold is expected to be reached, or has been reached, within any 12-month rolling period.

## Swiss VAT Rates (2026)

Switzerland applies three VAT rates:

| Rate | Type | Applies to |
|---|---|---|
| 8.1% | Standard rate | Most goods and services |
| 2.6% | Reduced rate | Food, non-alcoholic beverages, medicines, books, newspapers, magazines, agricultural inputs |
| 3.8% | Special accommodation rate | Hotel and accommodation services |
| 0% | Zero rate | Exports, international transport services |

The standard rate increased from 7.7% to 8.1% on 1 January 2024 as part of AHV financing reform. Switzerland's VAT rate remains the lowest among European countries — the EU minimum is 15%, and most EU countries apply rates of 20–25%.

## How to Register for Swiss VAT

Registration is handled by the <a href="https://www.estv.admin.ch/estv/en/home/mehrwertsteuer/mwst-eintragen.html" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Swiss Federal Tax Administration (ESTV)</a>.

### Step 1: Confirm registration is required

Before registering, confirm that the CHF 100,000 threshold has been reached or will be reached within the next 12 months. If you are uncertain, voluntary registration (see below) may still be advantageous.

### Step 2: Register online via the ESTV portal

Access the ESTV ePortal and complete the VAT registration form. You will need:

- Company details (UID number, name, registered address)
- Details of the taxable activities and expected turnover
- Start date of VAT liability (the date you first exceeded or expect to exceed CHF 100,000)
- Bank account details for refund purposes

For a Swiss-registered company, the UID is taken from the Commercial Register entry on <a href="https://www.zefix.ch" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">ZEFIX</a>.

### Step 3: Receive your VAT number

The ESTV processes the registration and issues a VAT number. This takes the form of the UID with the suffix "-MWST" (e.g. CHE-123.456.789 MWST). Processing typically takes 1 to 4 weeks.

### Step 4: Begin issuing VAT-compliant invoices

From the registration effective date, all invoices to taxable customers must include:

- Your VAT number
- The applicable VAT rate and amount
- The net and gross amounts

## VAT for Foreign Companies

Swiss VAT applies based on the place of supply, not the supplier's domicile. Foreign companies that supply goods or services in Switzerland may have Swiss VAT obligations even without a Swiss subsidiary.

**Mandatory registration for foreign companies:**

A foreign company must register for Swiss VAT if its worldwide taxable turnover exceeds CHF 100,000 and it provides taxable supplies in Switzerland. The Swiss address where goods are delivered or services are received determines the place of supply for most transactions.

**Since 1 January 2018:** Foreign e-commerce sellers that deliver goods to Switzerland and exceed CHF 100,000 in worldwide turnover must register, regardless of whether their individual deliveries are below the small-shipment import threshold.

**Since 2024:** Foreign platform operators (marketplace models) have additional registration and collection obligations for sales to Swiss customers.

**Fiscal representative requirement:**

Foreign companies without a Swiss establishment must appoint a fiscal representative (Fiskalvertretung) domiciled in Switzerland. The fiscal representative acts as the local contact for the ESTV and is jointly responsible for VAT compliance. Fiscal representation is typically provided by Swiss accountants or law firms and costs CHF 1,200 to CHF 3,600/year depending on filing volumes.

## Voluntary VAT Registration

Businesses below the CHF 100,000 threshold can register for VAT voluntarily. This is worthwhile when:

- **Input VAT recovery is significant.** A company purchasing substantial goods or services in Switzerland that include Swiss VAT can recover that input tax once registered. For a startup purchasing CHF 60,000 of taxable services, recovering CHF 4,860 in input VAT (at 8.1%) may justify early registration.

- **B2B customers require a VAT number.** Some Swiss corporate clients require a VAT number on invoices. Voluntary registration resolves this.

- **International businesses need Switzerland-registered VAT status.** Certain cross-border contracts or procurement relationships require it.

Voluntary registration subjects the company to all standard compliance obligations (quarterly returns, record-keeping, ESTV correspondence). Once registered, deregistration is possible only if turnover stays below CHF 100,000 for three consecutive calendar years and no voluntary continuation has been elected.

## VAT Filing and Payment Obligations

### Quarterly filing (standard)

VAT returns are filed quarterly for most registrants. The return covers the period January–March, April–June, July–September, and October–December. Filing and payment are due within 60 days of the end of the quarter.

Returns are submitted online via the ESTV ePortal. Each return reports:

- Total taxable turnover for the period, broken down by rate
- Input VAT claimed on purchases
- Balance due to the ESTV or refund claimed

### Annual filing

Annual filing is available for businesses with annual turnover below CHF 5,005,000 and VAT liability below CHF 103,000/year. The annual return is due by 28 February of the following year, with a single annual payment.

### Net tax rate method (Saldosteuersatz)

An alternative filing method is available for small businesses. Instead of tracking all input VAT individually, the company applies a pre-approved flat net rate (Saldosteuersatz) that represents the typical input VAT ratio for its industry. This simplifies record-keeping but cannot be combined with standard input VAT deduction.

### Late filing and payment penalties

Late VAT returns attract a reminder fee. Late payment of VAT due results in default interest at 4% per annum from the due date. Deliberate non-compliance carries higher penalties and potential criminal liability.

## Common VAT Mistakes in Switzerland

**1. Failing to register at the right time.** The registration obligation starts from the date the CHF 100,000 threshold is first reached (or expected to be reached). Late registration triggers back-charges of VAT on all supplies since the threshold was crossed, plus interest.

**2. Applying the wrong VAT rate.** The boundary between the standard 8.1% and reduced 2.6% rate is not always obvious. Food sold in a restaurant is at 8.1%; the same food sold in a shop is at 2.6%. Professional advice on rate classification prevents costly corrections.

**3. Recovering input VAT on non-deductible expenses.** Input VAT on entertainment expenses (above 50% private use), luxury goods, and certain motor vehicles is not fully deductible. Overclaiming leads to corrections and interest.

**4. Missing foreign company registration triggers.** Foreign companies often discover their Swiss VAT obligations late — sometimes only when audited. The worldwide turnover test means registration is required before reaching CHF 100,000 in Swiss-specific revenue.

**5. Inadequate invoice records.** Every input VAT claim requires a VAT-compliant supplier invoice. Missing or incomplete invoices lead to rejected input claims during ESTV audits.
