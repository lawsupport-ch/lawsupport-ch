A Swiss nominee director satisfies the legal requirement that at least one director with individual signatory rights be domiciled in Switzerland. The standard annual cost is CHF 5,900. The arrangement is legally sound when properly documented, widely used by foreign-owned Swiss companies, and carries real risks for both parties that are manageable with the right contractual structure. This guide explains what a nominee director does, what it costs in full, and how to assess a provider.

---

## Why a Nominee Director Is Needed in Switzerland

Article 718 of the <a href="https://www.fedlex.admin.ch/eli/cc/27/317_321_377/en" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Swiss Code of Obligations</a> requires that at least one managing director of a Swiss GmbH with individual signatory rights be domiciled in Switzerland. For AGs, the same principle applies to the board of directors under Article 707 CO.

"Domiciled" means having a genuine Swiss residence address registered with the relevant municipal authority. A registered office address, a postal address, or a director who visits Switzerland regularly does not satisfy this requirement.

For foreign founders who do not live in Switzerland — and who do not have Swiss-resident employees senior enough to serve as directors — a nominee director is the standard solution. It is not a workaround or a loophole. It is a standard professional service offered by Swiss lawyers, fiduciaries, and corporate services firms.

---

## What a Swiss Nominee Director Does — and Does Not Do

**What the nominee does:**

- Appears in the Commercial Register as a managing director or board member
- Holds individual signatory rights on behalf of the company
- Receives and forwards official correspondence
- Signs the annual financial statements and other statutory documents (typically included in the base fee)
- Signs the commercial register notifications when directors change
- Acts in accordance with lawful instructions from the beneficial owner

**What the nominee does not do (without additional fees and review):**

- Sign banking documents, mandate forms, or new account applications
- Attend bank compliance meetings
- Sign employment contracts, work permits, or applications to cantonal authorities
- Execute real estate or financial transactions
- Take any action without instructions from the beneficial owner
- Sign anything that has not been reviewed for the nominee's own legal exposure

The distinction matters. Founders sometimes assume a nominee is available on demand for any corporate action. In practice, each instruction beyond the base scope is reviewed individually, billed separately, and may require an indemnity from the beneficial owner.

## Legal Framework and the Nominee Agreement

A properly structured nominee arrangement rests on two documents:

**1. The nominee agreement (Dienstleistungsvertrag)**

A contract between the nominee and the beneficial owner specifying:
- The scope of the nominee's authority
- The limits of the nominee's authority (what the nominee will not do without explicit instruction)
- The nominee's indemnification by the beneficial owner for acts taken in good faith on instruction
- The conditions for termination

**2. The declaration of trust or powers of attorney**

Some providers use a signed declaration from the nominee that the beneficial owner is the true owner of the shares, or a general power of attorney from the beneficial owner to the nominee (or vice versa). These documents define the relationship between legal and economic ownership.

The nominee arrangement must comply with Swiss AML law. The nominee is treated as a financial intermediary for AML purposes (Article 2 AMLA) and must conduct their own due diligence on the beneficial owner's identity and source of funds. A nominee who accepts a mandate without performing adequate KYC exposes themselves to regulatory sanctions.

## Risks for Both Parties

### For the beneficial owner

The nominee holds formal legal authority over the company. Risks:

- A negligent nominee may sign documents without adequate review, causing legal or financial harm
- A nominee firm that closes or becomes insolvent may complicate the company's register position
- A nominee who refuses to sign a required document (because it exceeds their risk appetite) can delay critical transactions

**Mitigation:** Choose a nominee who is a licensed Swiss fiduciary or lawyer, operating under regulatory supervision. Ensure the nominee agreement clearly limits what the nominee can and cannot do.

### For the nominee

The nominee is legally a director — with full director-level liability:

- Regulatory exposure if the company breaches Swiss law (even if the nominee was not involved in the decision)
- Civil liability to creditors if the company trades while insolvent
- Criminal liability in cases of fraud or intentional misconduct

This is why reputable nominees conduct thorough KYC, decline mandates involving regulated activities without proper licences, and restrict their signatory authority for high-risk transactions.

## How to Choose a Nominee Director Provider

**1. Regulatory status.** The nominee should be a licensed Swiss fiduciary (eidgenössisch anerkannter Revisor, or member of a cantonal fiduciary association) or a Swiss lawyer. Regulated status means the nominee is supervised and has professional liability insurance.

**2. Written nominee agreement.** Any provider who cannot produce a clear, written nominee agreement defining the scope and limits of their authority should not be used.

**3. Transparency on additional costs.** Ask specifically what is and is not covered in the base annual fee. Vague answers are a warning sign.

**4. Experience with your business type.** Nominees specialising in holding companies have different risk profiles than nominees serving fintech startups or trading companies. Match the nominee to your specific industry.

**5. Capacity.** A nominee who is director of 50+ companies simultaneously may have insufficient attention for your company's specific needs. Ask about their portfolio size.

## Transitioning Away from a Nominee

Most founders plan to replace the nominee director within six to twelve months — once a Swiss employee is hired, or once a co-founder relocates. In practice, the transition consistently takes longer.

**Process:**

1. Appoint the new director at a shareholders' meeting or general meeting
2. File the director change with the cantonal Commercial Register (CHF 200–400)
3. Update the bank mandate (bank's own process, typically 2–4 weeks)
4. Obtain any regulatory notifications or client consent letters required by the company's activities
5. The nominee formally resigns — their name is removed from the register upon confirmation of the new director's entry

The transition itself is procedurally straightforward. What creates delays:

- Banking relationships where the bank must conduct fresh KYC on the new director
- Ongoing transactions that require the nominee's signatory authority during the period
- Accounting and tax filings signed under the nominee's authority that need transitioning to the new director

One German tech founder who planned a five to six month interim discovered the actual transition from nominee to self-directed company took over 14 months, because two banking relationships required fresh compliance reviews that ran concurrently with a commercial lease renewal.
