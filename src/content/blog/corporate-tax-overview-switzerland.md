Switzerland taxes corporate profits at two levels: federal and cantonal/communal. The federal rate is a flat 8.5% on net profit (effective 7.83% after self-deduction). Cantonal and communal rates vary widely — from about 3.5% in Zug to over 14% in Geneva — producing combined effective rates between 11.8% and 21.6%. This dual structure is the reason canton selection matters as much as legal structure when setting up a company in Switzerland.

This page brings together the key elements of Swiss corporate taxation: the federal layer, cantonal differences, capital tax, return filing, and how to choose the right location. Each section links to a detailed article for readers who need the full picture.

---

## How Swiss Corporate Tax Works

The Swiss tax system operates on three tiers: federal (Bund), cantonal (Kanton), and communal (Gemeinde). Corporate income tax is assessed at all three levels, though cantonal and communal taxes are administered together by the cantonal tax authority.

Every AG, GmbH, cooperative, association, and foundation with its registered office or place of effective management in Switzerland is subject to unlimited tax liability. Foreign companies with a permanent establishment or real estate in Switzerland face limited tax liability on Swiss-sourced income.

The tax base is net profit as shown in the statutory financial statements, adjusted for non-deductible expenses and tax-exempt income. Swiss GAAP and the Code of Obligations (OR) govern the preparation of these statements. For a thorough treatment of how corporate tax rates work across Switzerland, see our guide to [corporate tax in Switzerland](/tax-accounting/corporate-tax-switzerland/).

## Federal Corporate Tax

The Confederation levies a flat 8.5% tax on net profit. Because this tax is itself deductible from the tax base, the effective federal rate is approximately 7.83%. This rate has remained unchanged since 1998 and applies uniformly — there is no progressive scale for companies.

Key points about federal corporate tax:

- **Tax base**: Net profit per statutory accounts, adjusted for non-deductible items
- **Deductible**: Federal tax is deductible from its own base
- **Loss carry-forward**: Seven years, no carry-back
- **Minimum tax**: None at federal level — loss-making companies pay zero
- **Filing**: Through the cantonal tax authority, which forwards the federal portion

The Federal Tax Administration (<a href="https://www.estv.admin.ch/en" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">ESTV/AFC</a>) oversees the system, but day-to-day administration happens at cantonal level.

## Cantonal and Communal Tax Rates

Each of Switzerland's 26 cantons sets its own corporate income tax rate and multiplier. Communes within each canton apply an additional multiplier. The result is that two companies in the same canton but different communes may face different effective rates.

Cantonal tax structures fall into two categories:

1. **Flat-rate cantons**: Most cantons now apply a single rate to all profit levels. Zug, Lucerne, Nidwalden, and Obwalden use this approach.
2. **Progressive cantons**: A handful of cantons still apply mildly progressive rates, though the trend is towards flat taxation.

The cantonal tax is typically expressed as a percentage of "simple tax" (einfache Steuer), multiplied by a cantonal coefficient and a communal coefficient. This layered calculation makes direct comparison between cantons non-trivial. Our [cantonal tax comparison](/tax-accounting/cantonal-tax-comparison-switzerland/) provides a side-by-side breakdown of all 26 cantons.

## Capital Tax

In addition to income tax, Swiss cantons levy a [capital tax](/tax-accounting/capital-tax-switzerland/) on the equity of legal entities. The tax base is the company's taxable equity — share capital, legal reserves, retained earnings, and hidden reserves where identified by the authority.

Capital tax rates are modest. Most cantons charge between 0.01% and 0.5% of taxable equity. Several cantons — including Zurich and Lucerne — allow companies to offset income tax paid against their capital tax liability, effectively reducing or eliminating the capital tax for profitable companies.

There is no federal capital tax. The tax exists only at cantonal and communal level.

For holding companies with substantial equity, capital tax can become material. A company with CHF 50 million in equity in a canton charging 0.3% faces a CHF 150'000 annual capital tax bill regardless of whether it earns any income. This is one reason why holding structures often locate in cantons with capital tax offsets.

## Filing Corporate Tax Returns

Swiss companies must file a [corporate tax return](/tax-accounting/corporate-tax-return-switzerland/) for each financial year. The return covers both federal and cantonal/communal tax and is submitted to the cantonal tax authority where the company has its registered office.

The filing package typically includes:

- Completed tax return forms (cantonal and federal)
- Annual financial statements (balance sheet, income statement, notes)
- Supporting schedules (depreciation, provisions, intercompany transactions)
- List of shareholders (where required by the canton)
- Transfer pricing documentation (for companies with cross-border related-party transactions)

**Deadlines** vary by canton. Most cantons set the filing deadline at 6-9 months after the financial year-end. Companies with a 31 December year-end typically face deadlines between 30 June and 30 September. Extensions are routinely granted upon written request — some cantons allow extensions of up to 18 months.

**Penalties** for late filing start with reminder fees (CHF 50-200) and escalate to estimated assessments if no return is filed. Estimated assessments are intentionally set above the likely actual liability, with interest running from the original due date.

## Common Deductions and Adjustments

The tax return allows a range of deductions that reduce the taxable base:

- **Business expenses**: All ordinary and necessary business expenses are deductible, including salaries, rent, materials, and professional fees
- **Depreciation**: Statutory depreciation per OR rules plus tax depreciation per cantonal guidelines (which may differ)
- **Provisions**: For genuine, quantifiable liabilities — not speculative reserves
- **Losses carried forward**: Up to seven prior years, no carry-back
- **Charitable donations**: Up to 20% of net profit for qualifying recipients
- **Federal tax paid**: Deductible from the federal tax base

Non-deductible items include dividends paid, fines and penalties, private expenses of shareholders, and excessive compensation to related parties. Transfer pricing adjustments are the most common area of dispute between taxpayers and authorities — see our guide to [transfer pricing in Switzerland](/tax-accounting/transfer-pricing-switzerland/) for details.

## Holding Company Exemptions

Switzerland's participation deduction (Beteiligungsabzug) is one of the most important features of the corporate tax system. It effectively eliminates income tax on qualifying dividends and capital gains received from subsidiaries.

The deduction applies when:

- The parent holds at least 10% of the subsidiary's share capital, OR
- The participation has a fair market value of at least CHF 1 million

The relief is calculated as a proportional reduction of total tax, not as a deduction from the tax base. This means it can reduce the effective tax on qualifying income to near zero.

For companies considering Switzerland as a holding location, the combination of participation deduction, extensive treaty network, and competitive cantonal rates makes the country a strong candidate. ## Choosing the Right Canton

Canton selection is the single most impactful tax planning decision for a new Swiss company. The difference between the highest and lowest cantonal rate exceeds 10 percentage points — on CHF 1 million in profit, that gap represents over CHF 100'000 in annual tax savings.

Factors beyond the headline rate include:

- **Capital tax treatment**: Does the canton allow income tax offset?
- **Patent box generosity**: Cantons implement the patent box with varying levels of benefit
- **Municipal multiplier**: Rates vary within cantons, sometimes substantially
- **Personal tax**: If shareholders or directors live in the canton, personal tax rates matter
- **Workforce availability**: Low-tax cantons may have smaller talent pools
- **Infrastructure**: Office space, transport links, proximity to clients

Zug and Lucerne dominate the low-tax ranking but are not necessarily the best choice for every business. A manufacturing company needing warehouse space may find better value in Thurgau or Aargau. A financial services firm may prefer Zurich's infrastructure despite its higher rate. Consult our [cantonal tax comparison](/tax-accounting/cantonal-tax-comparison-switzerland/) for the full picture.

## Practical Steps for New Companies

For a company newly incorporated in Switzerland, the tax sequence proceeds as follows:

1. **Registration**: The cantonal tax authority is notified automatically when the company is entered in the Commercial Register
2. **First tax period**: Runs from the date of incorporation to the chosen financial year-end
3. **Provisional assessment**: Most cantons issue a provisional tax invoice based on projected income. This is payable during the year
4. **First return**: Due 6-9 months after the first financial year-end
5. **Final assessment**: Issued after the return is reviewed — may differ from the provisional invoice

New companies with no income still have filing obligations. A nil return must be submitted. Capital tax applies from day one, based on paid-in share capital.

Companies planning to take advantage of tax incentives — patent box, notional interest deduction, or step-up — should seek a [tax ruling](/tax-accounting/tax-ruling-switzerland/) from the cantonal authority before or shortly after incorporation. Pre-ruling certainty is standard practice in Switzerland and prevents surprises at assessment time.
